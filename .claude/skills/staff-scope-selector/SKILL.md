# Staff Scope Selector

## Purpose

The step from senior to staff+ is the step from "execute what's assigned"
to "choose what's worth doing" — and it's where strong engineers stall,
either firefighting reactively, chasing shiny tech with no impact, or
doing excellent senior-level work that never adds up to staff-level
leverage. This skill helps a staff+ IC (or one aspiring) select scope
deliberately: find the org's important, under-owned problems; match them
to genuine leverage (work that multiplies other engineers, sets
direction, or solves the hard thing everyone avoids) and to the person's
strengths; dodge the traps that make busy people low-impact; and commit
to a scope with a stated rationale and an explicit list of what they're
NOT doing. It's about choosing the work, not ranking a backlog or
documenting impact already delivered.

## Use When

- Use when: a staff+/senior IC is choosing what to work on next and wants
  the highest-leverage scope, not just the next ticket.
- Use when: someone's impact feels scattered, reactive, or below their
  level despite working hard.
- Use when: picking a next big bet / area of ownership at staff+ level.
- Use when: an aspiring-staff engineer needs to find scope that
  demonstrates staff-level leverage.
- Do NOT use when: the task is ranking a given team backlog by value/
  effort — that is `prioritization-frame-picker`.
- Do NOT use when: the task is planning the TEAM's roadmap over horizons —
  that is `roadmap-under-uncertainty-planner`; this is one person's scope.
- Do NOT use when: the task is documenting impact ALREADY delivered for a
  promotion case — that is `promotion-packet-writer`; this chooses FUTURE
  scope.

## Inputs to Inspect

1. The person's level and trajectory: current level, the level's
   expectations for scope/leverage, and whether they're aiming to grow
   into the next.
2. Their strengths and interests: what they're unusually good at and drawn
   to — leverage is highest where strength meets org need.
3. The org's important problems: the high-value problems, especially the
   under-owned ones nobody is driving.
4. The current portfolio: what they're working on now, and whether it's
   leverage or busy-ness (firefighting, shiny work, invisible glue).
5. Ownership boundaries: who owns what, so chosen scope fills a gap rather
   than over-reaching into someone else's area.

## Workflow

1. **Anchor on level-relative leverage.** Scope is judged against the
   level: staff+ impact multiplies others or moves the org, it isn't just
   more/harder individual output. Name what leverage means at this level
   before choosing.
2. **Find the important under-owned problems.** The best staff scope is
   usually a high-value problem nobody owns — the gap between teams, the
   decision nobody's making, the system everyone complains about but no
   one drives. List candidates.
3. **Match to leverage archetypes and strengths.** Map candidates to real
   leverage: multiplier work (unblock/enable many engineers),
   direction-setting (the technical strategy others build on), the hard
   problem others avoid, or critical glue. Weight by where the person's
   strengths fit — impact is highest at that intersection.
4. **Screen against the traps.** Reject or fix scope that is: only-fun
   (shiny tech, low impact), only-firefighting (reactive, no direction),
   too narrow for the level (senior work relabeled), invisible glue
   (valuable but with no attribution path — add visibility or reconsider),
   or over-reach into someone's owned scope (align first).
5. **Choose deliberately with a rationale.** Pick the scope and state WHY
   (leverage × fit × org need) — and, as importantly, state what you're
   NOT doing, so the focus is real. A staff engineer who owns everything
   owns nothing.
6. **Make the scope legible.** Ensure the chosen work is visible and its
   impact will be attributable (the glue-work trap is invisibility). This
   also feeds a future `promotion-packet-writer` case — but that's a
   separate, later job.
7. **Deliver** the scope selection in the Output Format: the chosen scope,
   the rationale, the leverage type, the fit, and the explicit non-scope.

The staff archetypes, the leverage-vs-busy-ness test, the trap catalog,
and the scope-rationale format:
[references/staff-scope-sheet.md](references/staff-scope-sheet.md).

## Output Format

```
STAFF SCOPE SELECTION — <person/level>
Level leverage:  what impact means at this level (multiplies others / sets direction)
Candidates:      important under-owned problems considered
CHOSEN scope:    <the scope> — leverage type=<multiplier|direction|hard-problem|glue>; fit=<strength>
Rationale:       leverage × fit × org-need — why this over the others
NOT doing:       <explicit non-scope, so focus is real>
Trap check:      not only-fun / not only-firefighting / not too-narrow / glue is visible / no over-reach
Legibility:      how the impact will be visible + attributable
Boundaries:      rank backlog → prioritization-frame-picker; team roadmap →
                 roadmap-under-uncertainty-planner; document past impact → promotion-packet-writer
```

## Validation Checklist

- [ ] Leverage is defined relative to the LEVEL (multiplies others / moves
      the org), not as more individual output.
- [ ] Candidate scope includes important UNDER-OWNED problems, not just
      what's already assigned.
- [ ] The chosen scope maps to a real leverage archetype AND the person's
      strengths.
- [ ] It's screened against the traps (only-fun, only-firefighting,
      too-narrow, invisible-glue, over-reach).
- [ ] The choice has an explicit rationale AND an explicit NOT-doing list.
- [ ] Invisible/glue scope has a visibility/attribution path or is
      reconsidered.
- [ ] Backlog-ranking, roadmap-planning, and promotion-documentation are
      handed to their owning skills.

## Gotchas

- Working hard is not the same as leverage. Firefighting all quarter feels
  productive and demonstrates no staff-level impact — it's senior
  execution on repeat. Direction and multiplication are the level.
- Shiny-tech scope (the rewrite in the trendy framework) is fun and
  usually low-leverage. Pick the boring high-impact problem over the
  exciting low-impact one.
- Glue work — the coordination, the unblocking, the unglamorous
  integration — is often the highest real leverage AND the most invisible.
  Do it, but build in the attribution, or it advances the org and not the
  engineer.
- Owning everything is owning nothing. Staff scope needs a NOT-doing list;
  without focus, the leverage dilutes into being spread thin.
- Over-reaching into another person's owned scope creates conflict and
  undermines them. Fill gaps and align on shared areas; don't colonize.
- Scope too narrow for the level (a well-executed feature) caps impact at
  senior no matter how well done. Match the ambition to the level.
- This chooses FUTURE scope; it doesn't document PAST impact. If you're
  assembling evidence of impact already delivered, that's
  `promotion-packet-writer`.

## Stop Conditions

- The task is ranking a given backlog → route to
  `prioritization-frame-picker`.
- The task is planning the team's roadmap → route to
  `roadmap-under-uncertainty-planner`.
- The task is documenting impact already delivered for promotion → route
  to `promotion-packet-writer`.
- The highest-leverage scope requires authority or headcount the person
  doesn't have, or entering someone else's owned area → surface it as an
  org/manager decision to align on rather than unilaterally claiming the
  scope.
